Project Hyper

Overview

Project Hyper is an effort to build a new artificial intelligence for massively multichannel digital advertising. It combines media planning, ad trafficking, and campaign optimization, and is built from the ground up to operate on top of existing buying platforms across a large number of highly differentiated supply channels. Project Hyper provides value across the entire spectrum of advertisers and agencies, helping small/medium advertisers implement the sophisticated trafficking and optimization techniques used by experienced ad traffickers, and allowing large advertisers and agencies to cope with the spiraling complexity of a massively multichannel environment.

Background

There are two important transformations currently underway in digital advertising:

  1. the move to "lowercase-p" programmatic – along with a slew of cost savings accruing from automation, the move to programmatic provides the ability to delay buy decisions until impression time (or close to it) and execute optimization strategies that mutate media plans on-the-fly; and
  2. the fragmentation of the supply landscape – we’re moving from a world with a small number of standardized channels to one with myriad proprietary channels born of apps, websites, and social networks, each with different ad units, targeting capabilities, buying mechanisms, and reporting granularity.

While these transformations provide a wealth of opportunity for great advertising, due in part to the diversity of devices and formats and contexts made available by the explosion of channels, they are also certain to increase the scope and complexity of the average campaign. The fundamental tactic for any well-executed programmatic campaign is some form of A/B or multi-variate testing – over several iterations high-performing micro-markets are identified and invested in and low-performing ones are retired. It’s not difficult to see that the complexity of managing this increases super-linearly with the number of channels. Furthermore, beyond the basic management task, new questions arise: How do I allocate testing budgets between channels? When and how can I apply the learnings from one channel to another? What are the secondary costs associated with moving budget between channels?

Ultimately, these transformations will change the nature of media planning, ad trafficking, and campaign optimization. Instead of discreet functions executed serially, they will merge into a single discipline that operates continually and iteratively from before the campaign goes live until the final impression is served.

While there has been an abundance of investment in programmatic ad systems, the design center for the vast majority of these efforts has been around real time bidding (RTB), cookie-based targeting, and standardized ad units, which have little to no support in the new proprietary supply channels. As such, the capabilities of these systems can only be applied on an increasingly small subset of available supply and the challenges of executing campaigns in this massively multichannel environment continues to worsen.

The Project Hyper Platform

The Project Hyper technology platform combines media planning, ad trafficking, and campaign optimization into a single, campaign-long, iterative activity. It operates on top of existing buying platforms – including traditional demand-side platforms (DSPs) and proprietary ad platforms such as Facebook Ads and Twitter Ads – taking advantage of the existing optimization capabilities within channels and adding new ones across them.

Centered around a new search-based AI, called the Hyperactive Engine, the platform employs a slew of components that are atypical of the current generation of ad tech, including a timeline-based, interactive UI, a multichannel allocation engine, a demand-side taxonomy, and graph-based normalization.

The Hyperactive Engine

The Hyperactive Engine is a new artificial intelligence built to address the challenges and opportunities of a massively multichannel environment. In contrast to the current generation of buy-side ad tech which has focused primarily on bid strategy, arbitrage, and user segmentation, Project Hyper's approach is one of anthropomorphic automation. The Hyperactive Engine seeks to emulate the strategies and tactics of the best human media planners and ad traffickers, but to do so with superhuman speed, memory, and foresight.

From a technical perspective, this new problem is the opposite of a big data problem. We have a large state space and sparse data. Hence, the solution is model-free and requires a search-based approach. The Hyperactive Engine uses a reinforcement learning approach.

To the end user, via the Hyper UI, the Hyperactive Engine is prescient and obsessively helpful. A simple campaign specification – budget, goals, creatives, and channels – is immediately expanded into a comprehensive multichannel media plan including a suite of A/B test iterations, timing and budget for exploration and later stage optimized execution, and configurable dials for biasing campaigns toward various goals, such as reach, conversion, or ad experience. Over the lifetime of a campaign, depending on size and scope, the Hyperactive Engine may generate dozens of iterations, from small configuration adjustments to entirely new exploration efforts.

The Hyper UI

The volume of activity and data generated by the Hyperactive Engine over a large number of channels can be overwhelming and so as part of Project Hyper we have designed a novel user interface, called the Hyper UI, that includes interactive campaign planning, a newsfeed-based notification system, and a series of timeline-based visualizations. The Hyper UI is an attempt to balance transparency and adjustability with automaticity.

Multichannel Allocation Engine

Conducting multitudes of experiments across many channels involves a litany of investment decisions. The Allocation Engine inside the Project Hyper platform uses operations-research-based techniques to control pacing and allocation of budget. It manages channel-based constraints (e.g. volume of supply) and user-specified constraints (e.g. minimum spends per channel) while optimizing for primary and secondary performance goals.

Graph-Based Normalization

Aggregating performance metrics (e.g. impressions, clicks, conversions) across channels is tiresome but straightforward. The more difficult but potentially more profitable challenge – a prerequisite for an effort such as Project Hyper – is to normalize placement and targeting attributes (e.g. age, education, interests). The heterogeneity of the current multichannel environment makes this particularly vexing. Project Hyper uses a graph-based data structure to understand soft and hard equivalencies between channels and make it possible to export learnings from one channel to all others.

Demand-Side Taxonomy

When great human ad planners/traffickers/optimizers make decisions about campaigns they do so through the lens of the advertiser. Experience has taught them that what works, where and when. As part of Project Hyper we are introducing a new taxonomy for describing advertisers, the products and services that are being advertised, and the creative assets used to advertise them.

Project Hyper is the product of a small group of ad tech contrarians whose minds are just sufficiently addled from their time manifesting three-letter acronyms to come up with such a hair-brained scheme.
